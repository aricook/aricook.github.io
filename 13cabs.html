I started off by talking to key stakeholders within the business to better understand their current needs, challenges and vision for the new website. Together we identified and aligned on expectations and were able to construct a shared overall vision of what the new site was encompass.

The new national 13cabs website was to aggregate all current customer and client facing websites and platforms into one.

Having decided on the vision projection, I began the research phase to better understand our audiences, competitors and product more deeply so we could deliver the best possible user centred product experience for all our users.

I started off by conducting a series of heuristic evaluations on the 5 websites we would be aggregating, this was to identify usability problems in compliance with Jakob Neilson’s 10 heuristic principles.

 

Websites analysed included;

• Cab Access; An online portal for operators to manage their fleet

• Seperate regional booking sites (Sydney, Newcastle, Melbourne, Adelaide, and Brisbane) as well as Personal and business portals used for booking and account management.

Key heuristic insights;

 

• Consistency of standards:

 

Content was outdated or found irreverent for section purpose as different states had different standards and requirements.13cabs also caters for people with disabilities, however their website rated very low in on the Australian Website Accessibility Standards.

• Prevention error:

 

Many sections of the site had repetition of content, leading users in circles on the site.

 

 

 

• Design and aesthetics:

 

All states in which 13cabs serviced, had dissimilar content and design aesthetics.

• Recognition, not recall:

 

Business customers had several URL’s and logins for different purposes

 

 

 

• Flexibility:

 

4 out of 5 sites were not mobile responsive

discovery.

After conducting a review on the numerous 13cabs websites, I conducted a competitive analysis with 13cabs 2 main customer focus areas; business and personal travellers. I set out to discover the best and worst practices within the competitive landscape to see how 13cabs could strengthen its value offering within the market.

 

Key insight;

13cabs main value offer amongst its competitors were;

• Ability to hail a taxi

• No surcharges

• Wheelchair accessible vehicles

• Maxi Taxis

• Number of cabs available for passengers


Despite these high value benefits, the competitors were perceived as market leaders by providing their customers with up to date technology platforms with mobile friendly, easy to use, contemporary designs.

After completing the competitive analysis, I conducted a number of contextual inquiries with the user types that we perceived would be using the new site.

These high level users included travellers, operators (people who own the taxies) and drivers. I wanted to find out how these user types currently interact with the 13cab websites available to them, what they liked, what their pain points were and how we could better their experience as a traveller, operator or driver.

contextual inquiries

Travellers

Personal: Online via a mobile device as a guest, or hailed a cab.

Business: 13cabs services many types of business, both large and small. Concierge user types booked for travellers via the online business account (desktop) and paid for their accounts via the a separate website. Employees travelling for business usually booked online as a guest (mobile or desktop) or hailed a cab, and would pay for these trips using a fast card or e-ticket linked to the account.

Different user types were using the 13cabs websites on different platforms, for differing reasons;

Operators

Would mainly use the website on a desktop to perform more tedious tasks such as allocating their vehicles to drivers, viewing the location of their vehicles, and viewing the financials of their fleet.

 

Drivers

People who wanted to become drivers wanted to know why and how, via a mobile device. Drivers wanted to be given more updates-more often on their mobile devices, such as traffic updates, hot spots, vehicle logs, and financials.

Information Architecture

From our research and feature prioritisation, we concluded that we would pursue a mobile first product rollout for the following reasons;

 

• Data analytics: The current national mobile website did not serve nearly as good an experience as the desktop, meaning there were more gains to be made in relation to the UX.

 

• Effort: Developing mobile first would make the desktop development timeframe easier and quicker as we could e-use some of the features developed for mobile on desktop.

 

• Time: Mobile web would be based off the App, which has already undergone much research and testing, meaning we could confidently roll out the mobile web quickly, gaining more bookings in a shorter period of time.

 

• Content focused: The mobile-first approach organically leads to a design that’s more content and user focused for our region specific content.

Deciding on mobile first gave us the opportunity to decide on wether we would create an adaptive or responsive site. The decision was made to create an adaptive site due to the following considerations;

 

• Increased customer focus: We could gain more benefits from building a tailored experience for a mobile device; Tailored information based on users location services and a more interactive seamless booking and payment experience.

 

• Speed: Fast loading speeds are essential to retaining customers and improving their overall experience. An adaptive build would allow for a reduced size of the site which would in turn quicken the load speed.

UI & visual design

Knowing the vision, structure and flow of the product, I started to sketch low fidelity wireframes to ensure that the developers and stakeholders had a clear understanding of the project’s design.

 

After consulting the developers stake holders I adjusted the wireframes until ready for usability testing.

I tested the low fidelity wireframe flows to collect qualitative and quantitative data and determine the participant's overall satisfaction with the product. After gathering insights from over 10 testing sessions I iterated on the designs and continued the design-test-build cycle until we reached a high fidelity prototype with the best possible testing results and user centered design.

Sitting with the developing team I helped test and guide the product to deployment. We released the national website region by region, replacing and aggregating the old sites as we went.

 

 

Once the mobile MVP was released I synthesised feedback (from google analytics, heat maps, user feedback and video recordings) to help improve the experience, performance and conversion rates.

Key insights;

Through reviewing analytics received over the 1st month of going live we uncovered usability issues, then designed solutions against these problems. Some of the key issues uncovered were;

 

• Through observing over 20 videos alike we made the hypothesis that people were not noticing the booking tracking link provided via SMS, which caused users to book again.

Solution: This problem was raised as a priority and solved by adding a pop up to remind travellers how to track their booking. This was an MVP solution until the ‘my bookings’ section was added to the site.

 

• We found that users were clicking “request new code” or clicking the ‘back’ button before they received their verification code, which caused a mix up with verification codes entered resulting in users exiting the booking process. We hypothesised that this was happening due to the delay in receiving the SMS code when landing on the verification code screen.

Solution: This problem was raised as a priority and solved by;

1) Displaying back button after SMS was sent

2) Displaying the resend code link after a longer period of time

3) Prioritsing the  team to work on engineering the verification code to be sent to the user in a shorter time frame.

4) Keeping the same code if users clicked resend within a 1 min time frame.

the outcome

The biggest challenge in this project was aggregating a number of existing sites into an easy to use and learnable product for existing users returning to the site during multiple design iterations. I was able to overcome this by keeping all user types in mind when strategising what features we would release and when, as well as notifying key clients by emailing them about specific adjustments that could potentially affect their work processes. The final mobile design was a great success (desktop is yet to be released at the time of this writing).

 

The mobile website had received over 10,000 visits in the first 3 weeks of release, with a 96% successful booking conversion rate, up over 23% from the previous version.
